# Prototype

## What I built

I built a clickable prototype concept for **Relationship Intelligence OS**, an AI-native enterprise product for large financial-services relationship work.

The prototype demonstrates how fragmented signals from service, sales/advice, marketing, digital behavior, workplace activity, operations, documents, and risk workflows can be converted into one governed next-best action.

The hero scenario centers on a synthetic customer, **Alex Morgan**, who appears to be entering a retirement transition moment. The system connects omnichannel signals such as retirement-content engagement, workplace 401(k) activity, mobile usage, virtual assistant searches, secure messages, service calls, document defects, screen-share support, and an upcoming advisor consultation.

The prototype is designed to show that the product is not a chatbot or dashboard. It is an operating intelligence layer that moves from:

**Signals → Inference → Recommendation → Risk Review → Human Approval → Measurable Outcome**

## AI value archetype

Primary archetype: **Orchestrator**

Secondary archetype: **Oracle**

Relationship Intelligence OS acts as an Orchestrator because it connects signals across systems, interprets the customer journey, recommends action bundles, routes work to the right role, and requires human approval before customer-visible impact.

It also acts as an Oracle because it interprets weak signals across channels and explains why a relationship moment, service risk, or planning opportunity is emerging.

---

## Bet in one sentence

If financial-services firms can connect omnichannel relationship signals into governed, evidence-backed next-best actions, then service, advice, marketing, operations, and risk teams can act earlier, reduce rework, improve meeting readiness, and create better customer outcomes.

---

## What the prototype should prove

The prototype should prove that a user can understand the product’s core value in under 10 seconds:

**From fragmented touchpoints to trusted next action.**

It should also prove that the product can:

1. Detect a relationship moment before it becomes another disconnected service case.
2. Explain what signals drove the recommendation.
3. Recommend a concrete action bundle.
4. Show risk and control boundaries.
5. Require human approval before customer impact.
6. Connect the recommendation to measurable business outcomes.

---

## Kill criteria

This bet should be killed or materially narrowed if the prototype fails any of these tests:

1. Users interpret the product as a chatbot, dashboard, or CRM summary tool rather than an operating intelligence layer.
2. The 360-degree signal synthesis is not clear within the first 10 seconds.
3. The recommendation does not feel specific, credible, or action-oriented.
4. The evidence trail does not explain why the AI made the recommendation.
5. The trust and controls layer feels cosmetic instead of essential.
6. Users cannot identify which business metric should improve after the recommendation is approved.
7. The product cannot show a believable path to reducing repeat contact, document rework, associate effort, or advisor preparation gaps.
8. The product feels easy for Salesforce, Microsoft, ServiceNow, or another platform to copy as a simple feature.

---

## Success criteria

This bet becomes stronger if users can clearly say:

1. What signal the product detected.
2. Why the signal matters.
3. What action the product recommends.
4. What evidence supports the recommendation.
5. What risk or approval gate applies.
6. What outcome should improve.

The strongest success signal is not that users find the interface attractive. The strongest signal is that they understand how the product changes the work.
